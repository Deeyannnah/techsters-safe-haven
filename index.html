Why we go out of our way to help

We condemn the lack of respect and safety for girl children and refuse to stand by as they are constantly treated like third-class citizens. So, we act for these reasons.

No Health Benefit

The practice of female genital mutilation (FGM) can cause severe bleeding and problems urinating, as well as cysts, infections, as well as complications in childbirth and newborn deaths. It can also lead to increased risks of HIV transmission.

Forced Marriage Is Abuse

Child and forced marriage(CFM) ends childhood abruptly. It also adversely affects children's rights to education, health, and protection, and puts them at risk of violence, exploitation, and abuse. These consequences affect not only the girl but also her family and community.

Psychological Impact

It can have psychological impacts in several ways, from a girl losing trust in her caregivers and parents to longer-term feelings of anxiety and depression. Thereby putting her at risk of suicide.

How we help?

We think that girls' voices can drive change, not just in the future but also in the here and now. Our programming strategy gives young girls all around the world the power to make the choices that will affect their lives and the communities in which they live.

1

Shielding the girl child

"Shielding" refers to the measures we take to stop or prevent the abuse of children and young people, including empowering staff to report and address concerns in order to make everyone safer.

2

Surveillance and Assessment

To make sure that our initiatives to support girls in these areas are efficient, significant, and accountable, we strive to set up monitoring and evaluation mechanisms in communities where these behaviors are widespread.

3

Awareness

We put up campaigns to educate people and include entire communities, both online and off, with an emphasis on human rights, gender equality, sexual education, and the needs of women and girls who experience its effects.

4

Legislative Aid

We connect victims with legislative aid to educate them on their human rights and offer them legal advice in the case where it is needed.

5

Medical and Psychological Evaluation

While understanding that victims suffer from various issues ranging from infections to traumas, we try our best to connect them to both medical and psychological help as a way to help them heal and lead a happier life.

Impact Stories

FGM discussion is a vital part of our community, but only the testimony of the impacted women can convey the actual pain of this practice. African women's personal narratives are used to accomplish this. We present a few heartbreaking tales below to humanize the victims and shed light on this heinous practice:

say NO to Female Genital Mutilation in Africa.
Yaam
22 years old
Yaam 22 years old

My sisters and I don't ever commiserate about what happened to us because I guess we are all trying to repress the horrible memories of it. My father never wanted this for us, which is why they would always wait for him to be out of town for work before they'd take us. I heard from a relative that when my older sister and I were taken, my dad and uncle tried to press charges against the family members who did it. But instead, they had a community meeting and settled on, "What's done is done." I hope that some day soon, I will be able to talk to my dad about what happened to me without causing any trouble.

Aisha
33 years old
Aisha 33 years old

My sister came to the U.S. for her education, and she was the one who helped me come here as well. She cannot have kids as a result of her FGM. I have two daughters now and had to be cut open again to have each one. But I had them here, with a doctor. Back in Gambia, I could have died. My girls will be the only ones in my family who will not have to go through FGM. I know if I ever have my daughters in Gambia, they will do to them what they did to me. My mother assured me of this. I will never take them back. My family will never see them.

Lesha
21 years old
Lesha 21 years old

I went to Africa to learn about my identity just to end up being scarred for life. I didn't realize FGM was a part of my identity, and to this day that's what hurts the most. My hope is for survivors of FGM to have a safe place to go to without being judged. I also want a reconstructive surgery not just for me but for everyone like me.To the doctors reading this, please consider offering free surgeries for women like me. It would change our lives. And I hope the government takes action and raises awareness like they should. I don't think I can talk about this again, but I hope others speak up.

Ruana
19 years old
Ruana 19 years old

“I was infibulated when I was five years old. It hurt so much that I cried and cried. When I was nearly twelve, my aunts one day examined me. They declared that I was not closed enough. They took me to the midwife who lives a few streets away. Then they cut me again; and this time, the woman who operated on me made sure that I was closed. It is years later now. The doctors told me that I could never have children because of the infibulation. Therefore, no one will marry me; no one wants a wife who cannot have a child. I sit at home alone and I cry a lot. I look at my mother and my aunts, and I ask them: ’Why did you do this terrible thing to me?’.
